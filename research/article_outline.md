## 1. Lead (75–100 words)

The hook needs to puncture the consensus framing.

Suggested opening:
> On February 4, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission announced what it called a "landmark" settlement with Express Scripts. Headlines said the agency had blown up the pharmacy-benefit-manager business model. Reading the actual 15-page consent decree, that's not quite right. The FTC didn't blow up the model — it made the existing model the *opt-out option*. Whether anyone actually opts in is now the entire story, and a single sentence in Section XI tells you why.

**One number for the section:** The "$7B in patient savings over 10 years" headline figure.

**Source:** [FTC press release, Feb 4, 2026](https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2026/02/ftc-secures-landmark-settlement-express-scripts-lower-drug-costs-american-patients)

## 2. The Story So Far (100 words)

Quick recap so newer readers aren't lost. Don't dwell.

Cover:
- FTC sued the Big Three PBMs and their offshore rebate aggregators in September 2024 over insulin pricing.
- ESI/Ascent settled February 4, 2026.
- Caremark/Zinc filed joint motion to withdraw from adjudication on March 23, 2026 — settlement coming.
- OptumRx/Emisar still negotiating as of April 2026.
- The Big Three PBMs process roughly **80% of all U.S. prescription claims**. Express Scripts alone has **123.6 million pharmacy customers** as of December 2025 and processes **30% of U.S. prescription claims**, up from 23% in 2023 (a jump driven by the January 2024 Centene win).

**One number for the section:** 80% of all U.S. prescription claims processed by the Big Three.

## 3. What's Actually Locked In (200 words)

The pieces of the order with no opt-out. This is the heart of the piece.

Cover four things:

**Section VI — No compensation tied to list price.** The structural change. As of January 1, 2027, ESI's compensation from drug manufacturers — including the "administrative fees" and "data fees" historically used to launder rebates through Ascent — cannot be based, directly or indirectly, on list price. This breaks the rebate-as-percent-of-WAC math that has driven a decade of list-price inflation. No plan-sponsor opt-out, no carve-outs.

**Section X — Ascent reshore + GPO Safe Harbor.** By July 1, 2028, all Ascent rebate-negotiation activities, employees, functions, and assets move from Schaffhausen, Switzerland (Mühlentalstrasse 36, the actual address is in the order) to the U.S. More substantively, any rebate GPO ESI owns must comply with the Anti-Kickback Statute's GPO Safe Harbor at 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952 — meaning written agreements with each participant, fee disclosure, and the standard fee-cap-or-disclosure structure that PBM-owned GPOs have long argued they were exempt from.

**Section IX — Mandatory marketing of the new "Standard Offering."** Unusually, the FTC requires ESI to spend **at least $10 million per year for five years** advertising and promoting the new transparent offering, with explicit no-disparagement and no-coercion provisions.

**Section II for Cigna's fully-insured book.** The single carve-in that survives Section XI: by the proviso in § XI.A, Cigna Healthcare's own fully-insured plans cannot opt out of the "member out-of-pocket cannot exceed net unit cost" rule. Cigna has roughly **18% of its commercial book in fully-insured plans** (the other 82% is self-insured ASO). So that 18% is hard-locked; the 82% can opt out.

## 4. The Loophole (200 words)

Section XI ("Meeting Competition") is the load-bearing piece. Walk the reader through it without making them feel dumb.

Cover:
- Sections II, III, IV, V, and VIII can all be opted out of by a written request from a plan sponsor.
- ESI must "make available" the Standard Offering and obtain written acknowledgement that the plan sponsor read Exhibit A. After that, anything goes.
- The asymmetry: if the Standard Offering is more expensive to ESI to operate (which it almost certainly is — passing rebates to members at point of sale, no spread pricing, cost-plus pharmacy reimbursement), ESI will price it at a premium. Plan sponsors will see two quotes and choose the cheaper one. The cheaper one will be the legacy model.
- This is precisely why § IX exists — the FTC forced ESI to spend $50 million over 5 years actively marketing the Standard Offering, because they knew the natural commercial gravity would push it into a corner of the proposal package.

**Quant frame:** of the roughly **123.6 million pharmacy customers** ESI serves, only the slice that's in Cigna Healthcare's fully-insured plans is hard-locked. Drug Channels and Cigna 10-K data suggest that's a single-digit percentage of ESI's total covered lives. The rest — most of the book — is opt-out. This is the math the Tribune piece needs to put in front of the reader.

**Visual:** **Chart 2 — ESI's covered lives, locked-in vs. opt-out.** Stacked horizontal bar showing roughly: Cigna fully-insured (locked) ~6–8%; Cigna self-insured (opt-out) ~30%; non-Cigna third-party plan sponsors (opt-out) ~62%. Note: precise breakdown isn't disclosed; flag estimation in the chart.

## 5. Where the $7B Comes From (150 words)

Disambiguation paragraph. The headline figure is overwhelmingly an insulin story.

Cover:
- Roughly **7.3 million U.S. adults use insulin**. Of those, **52% are Medicare**, **33% commercially insured**, **12% Medicaid**, and **2% uninsured** (CDC/HHS data). So the commercially insured insulin population — the only one ESI's commercial book touches — is roughly **2.4 million people**.
- Insulin gross-to-net divergence is the largest in pharma. Commercial discounts grew from **27% of gross sales in 2012 to 60.5% in 2019** for the four major insulins. List prices kept rising while net prices fell.
- The savings mechanism is § II: a high-deductible-plan member used to pay full *list* price on insulin until they hit deductible. Now they pay the *net* price (or the Patient Assurance Program cap, whichever is lower) from dollar one. With **27% of covered workers in HDHPs in 2024 (33% in 2025, KFF)**, the savings concentrate in the HDHP-on-insulin slice.
- After 2023's manufacturer-led list-price cuts (Lilly 70%, Sanofi 78%, Novo 65–75%), the gross-to-net gap has already narrowed considerably — meaning some of the FTC's projected $7B may already be baked in.

**Visual:** **Chart 3 — Insulin gross-to-net divergence, 2012–2024.** Two-line chart: list price index vs. net price index. Annotate the 2023 manufacturer cuts.

## 6. The Competitor-Pressure Mechanic (100 words)

Briefly explain why ESI settling first mattered for Caremark.

Cover:
- Section XVI: ESI must cooperate with FTC's continuing litigation against any other party. Specifically, must produce up to 5 employees for deposition and 3 for trial — at ESI's own cost.
- ESI is now contractually obligated to help the FTC against Caremark and OptumRx.
- Caremark filed its joint motion to withdraw on March 23, 2026 — six and a half weeks after ESI settled. The timing isn't coincidental.
- Most coverage treats § XVI as boilerplate. It isn't. It's leverage.

## 7. What to Watch (100 words)

The "what's next for readers who want to stay smart" close.

Three questions:

1. **When Caremark's order publishes, does it have the same Section XI architecture?** If yes, the loophole is industry-wide. If the FTC tightened it for Caremark, that's the bigger story.
2. **Does Congress or CMS act on the Section 1927/340B carve-out for direct-to-consumer pricing?** Without it, the decree's TrumpRx provision (§ III) is a paper provision. Watch for an NPRM from CMS or a bill moving through Senate Finance.
3. **What do plan sponsors actually do?** Aon, Mercer, and WTW survey their clients annually on PBM contracting. The first post-Implementation surveys (2027/2028) will reveal whether the Standard Offering is being adopted or opted out of.

End with a one-sentence kicker that lands the take.
