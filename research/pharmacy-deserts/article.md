<h1 class="article-headline">The pharmacy desert is a PBM business model</h1>

<p class="article-deck">Nearly one in three US retail pharmacies have closed since 2010, and the contraction is accelerating in 2026. Two FTC staff reports trace the squeeze to a small set of contract terms that move money from independents to pharmacies the PBMs own.</p>

<div class="article-byline">
  By <strong>Chris Olsen</strong><span class="dot">&middot;</span>May 31, 2026<span class="dot">&middot;</span>7 min read
</div>

<div class="article-body">

  <p class="lead">A pharmacy desert is what happens when the math stops working at the counter. <strong>29.4%</strong> of US retail pharmacies closed between 2010 and 2021, according to Guadamuz et al. in Health Affairs, and independents shut at more than twice the rate of chains. The FTC's two 6(b) staff reports on prescription drug middlemen, released July 9, 2024 and January 14, 2025, name the mechanism. Three Big 3 PBMs, Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx, reimburse unaffiliated pharmacies below acquisition cost, steer prescriptions to pharmacies they own, and mark up specialty generics at the affiliated channel by hundreds of percent. The result, per Berenbrok et al. in JAMA Network Open (March 2025), is that 57.1 million Americans now live somewhere a prescription is hard to fill.</p>

  <h2>How the math fails at the counter</h2>

  <p>An independent pharmacy spends roughly <strong>$12 to $15</strong> to dispense a single prescription, according to the National Community Pharmacists Association's Cost of Dispensing study (NCPA is the trade association for independent pharmacies, funder disclosed). PBM contracts routinely pay less than that on generic claims. The FTC Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen documents below-acquisition reimbursement directly, meaning the pharmacy loses money on the drug before it counts a minute of labor.</p>

  <p>This is not a marginal problem. It is the unit economics of the business. A store that fills 200 generic prescriptions a day at a two-dollar loss per script is bleeding $400 every day before rent. The contracts are take-it-or-leave-it because the Big 3 process roughly 80% of U.S. prescription claims, so refusing one network is refusing most of a town's insured customers.</p>

  <h2>Where the money goes instead</h2>

  <p>The FTC Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen quantifies the other side of the trade. Big 3 affiliated pharmacies generated <strong>$7.3 billion</strong> in revenue above estimated acquisition cost on specialty generic drugs during the study period. The same PBMs reimbursed their own pharmacies at higher rates than unaffiliated pharmacies on nearly every specialty generic studied, including cancer and HIV therapies. Spread pricing produced another $1.4 billion in profit, most of it on prescriptions dispensed at non-affiliated stores.</p>

  <p>The mechanism is the same across all three line items. The PBM sets the network price, owns the retail and specialty pharmacy that wins the network, and steers high-margin scripts there. The unaffiliated pharmacy fills the low-margin ones at a loss, or it loses the patient entirely to mail order.</p>

  <h2>The map of the loss</h2>

  <p>Berenbrok et al., writing in JAMA Network Open in March 2025, mapped a vulnerability index across the contiguous United States. <strong>57.1 million</strong> people, 17.7% of the population, live in a pharmacy desert. Another 28.9 million depend on a single pharmacy, what the authors call a keystone pharmacy. In 18 states more than 20% of residents live in a desert. In New Hampshire, South Dakota, West Virginia, Maine, and Vermont, the share exceeds 25%.</p>

  <p>These are not abstractions. A keystone pharmacy is one closure away from a desert. The Guadamuz et al. Health Affairs study from December 2024 found that independents, the stores most likely to be the keystone in a small town, closed at more than twice the rate of chains over the 2010 to 2021 window.</p>

  <h2>The 2026 acceleration</h2>

  <p>The closures are running faster this year than at any point in the post-2010 record. The American Economic Liberties Project's public closure tracker counted more than <strong>300 U.S. pharmacy closures in the first 10 weeks of 2026</strong>, of which 237 were independents, per NCPA Executive Updates on February 20 and March 6 (AELP is an anti-monopoly advocacy group funded by the Hewlett Foundation and Omidyar Network, funders disclosed; NCPA is the independent pharmacy trade association, funder disclosed). The 10-year net decline is roughly 8,000 stores, a 15% drop.</p>

  <p>No third FTC interim 6(b) report has appeared in the public docket as of May 2026. State PBM reform laws are moving instead. Florida HB 697, signed in 2026, lets pharmacists refuse to dispense at below-cost reimbursement and requires equal payment to unaffiliated pharmacies. Whether that floor slows the closure curve is an empirical question the data does not yet answer.</p>

  <div class="article-kicker">
    <span class="label">The bottom line</span>
    The pharmacy desert map and the PBM income statement describe the same trade. Below-acquisition reimbursement at the unaffiliated counter is the cash flow that funds specialty generic markups and spread at the affiliated counter. Watch two things over the next year. Whether Florida HB 697 and similar state laws bend the closure curve in their jurisdictions, and whether the FTC files a third 6(b) report or moves to a formal enforcement action against the contract terms its staff has already named.
