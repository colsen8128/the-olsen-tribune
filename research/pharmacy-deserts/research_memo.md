## TRIBUNE RESEARCH BRIEF

**Topic:** The economics driving independent pharmacy closures and the spread of pharmacy deserts across the United States.
**Date compiled:** May 17, 2026
**Confidence:** Medium — Primary FTC and peer-reviewed sources are solid through January 2025; closure counts for late 2025 and 2026 rely on trade-association tallies (NCPA, AELP) rather than a federal census, and dispensing-cost figures come from a pharmacist-funded study.

### Core finding
Nearly one in three U.S. retail pharmacies closed between 2010 and 2021, and the contraction has accelerated: more than 300 stores, the majority independent, have closed since December 2024. The FTC's two interim 6(b) staff reports trace the squeeze to three concrete PBM practices, below-acquisition reimbursement to unaffiliated pharmacies, steering of prescriptions to PBM-owned channels, and specialty-generic markups that produced $7.3 billion in excess revenue at affiliated pharmacies during the study period. The result is a geography of access: 17.7% of Americans in the contiguous U.S., about 57.1 million people, now live in a pharmacy desert.

### Key facts (verified)
- 29.4% of U.S. retail pharmacies closed between 2010 and 2021; independents were more than twice as likely to close as chains — Source: Guadamuz et al., "Retail Pharmacy Closures, 2010–2021," Health Affairs (USC Schaeffer release, December 3, 2024).
- 57.1 million people (17.7% of the contiguous U.S. population) live in a pharmacy desert; 28.9 million additionally rely on a single pharmacy — Source: Berenbrok et al., "Vulnerability Index Approach to Identify Pharmacy Deserts and Keystone Pharmacies," JAMA Network Open (March 2025).
- The Big 3 PBMs reimbursed affiliated pharmacies at higher rates than unaffiliated pharmacies on nearly every specialty generic studied; affiliated pharmacies generated $7.3 billion in revenue above estimated acquisition cost during the study period — Source: FTC, "Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen," 6(b) Study (January 14, 2025).
- The Big 3 PBMs generated an additional $1.4 billion in spread-pricing profit, most of it on prescriptions dispensed at non-affiliated pharmacies — Source: FTC, "Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen" (January 14, 2025).
- PBM contracts impose "unfair, arbitrary, and harmful" terms that affect independents' ability to stay in business — Source: FTC, "Pharmacy Benefit Managers: The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies," Interim Staff Report, 6(b) Study (July 9, 2024).
- More than 300 U.S. pharmacies, 237 of them independents, closed in roughly 10 weeks at the start of 2026 — Source: American Economic Liberties Project closure tracker, cited in NCPA Executive Update (February 20, 2026 and March 6, 2026).
- Dispensing a prescription costs an independent pharmacy roughly $12–$15; PBM contracts routinely reimburse below that — Source: NCPA, "Cost of Dispensing" study summary, cited in NCPA reimbursement materials (2025) (funder disclosed: NCPA is the trade association for independent pharmacies).
- In 18 states more than 20% of residents live in a pharmacy desert; in NH, SD, WV, ME, and VT the share exceeds 25% — Source: Berenbrok et al., JAMA Network Open (March 2025).

### Key players
- Caremark (CVS Health): largest PBM by claims volume; vertically integrated with CVS retail and Caremark mail/specialty pharmacies.
- Express Scripts (Cigna/Evernorth): second-largest PBM; affiliated with Accredo specialty pharmacy.
- OptumRx (UnitedHealth Group): third of the Big 3; affiliated with Optum specialty and home-delivery pharmacies. Combined, the Big 3 process ~80% of U.S. prescription claims.
- Federal Trade Commission: issuing agency of the 6(b) PBM study; staff reports authored under the chairmanships of Lina Khan (2024) and the FTC Office of Policy Planning (2025).
- National Community Pharmacists Association (NCPA): trade association for ~19,000 independent pharmacies; primary source for closure tracking and dispensing-cost data (funder disclosed: independent pharmacy owners).
- American Economic Liberties Project: anti-monopoly advocacy group maintaining the public pharmacy-closure tracker (funder disclosed: Hewlett Foundation, Omidyar Network, and others).
- Researchers Dima Mazen Qato and Jenny Guadamuz (USC Schaeffer): authors of the Health Affairs closure study.

### Open questions
- Exact closure split between independents and chains for full-year 2025 has not been verified against a federal census; trade-association numbers are the only available tally.
- Whether the FTC will issue a third interim report or a final 6(b) report in 2026 is not confirmed by an FTC docket entry as of May 2026.
- Whether 2026 state-level PBM reform laws (e.g., Florida HB 697) have measurably slowed independent closures in those jurisdictions — outcome data not yet published.
- The Health Affairs study's exact chain-vs-independent closure percentages should be pulled from the paper PDF before being bolded in the article.
- NCPA's $12–$15 cost-to-dispense figure is widely cited; the underlying methodology should be reviewed before treating it as a federal-quality estimate.
