Chart 1. Independents closed at more than twice the rate
U.S. retail pharmacy closures, 2010 to 2021, by ownership type
Nearly one in three U.S. retail pharmacies closed over the decade. Independents shut at more than twice the rate of chains, and were disproportionately the only pharmacy serving their community.
Chain pharmacies Independent pharmacies
All-retail figure (29.4%) is exact from Guadamuz et al. Chain and independent shares are illustrative approximations derived from the paper's finding that independents closed at more than twice the chain rate. Exact category percentages should be confirmed against the published paper before reuse.
Source: Guadamuz et al., "Retail Pharmacy Closures in the United States, 2010 to 2021," Health Affairs (December 2024).
Chart 2. Five states with the largest desert share
Share of state population living in a pharmacy desert
In five states more than a quarter of residents already live in a pharmacy desert, well above the 17.7% national average. Eighteen states sit above 20%.
State share in a pharmacy desert U.S. average (17.7%)
The five states named in Berenbrok et al. all exceed 25%; specific per-state percentages are approximated from the paper's bucketed reporting. The 17.7% national average is exact. Eighteen states (not all shown) exceed the 20% threshold.
Source: Berenbrok et al., "Vulnerability Index Approach to Identify Pharmacy Deserts and Keystone Pharmacies," JAMA Network Open (March 2025).
Chart 3. Where the Big 3 PBM dollars came from
PBM-affiliated pharmacy specialty-generic excess revenue and spread-pricing profit
Across the FTC study window, the Big 3 PBMs captured $7.3 billion in revenue above estimated acquisition cost at their own pharmacies, and an additional $1.4 billion in spread pricing, most of it on prescriptions filled at unaffiliated stores.
Specialty-generic excess at affiliated pharmacies Spread-pricing profit
FTC Second Interim Staff Report study window covers Big 3 PBM specialty-generic claims; both figures are exact from the report. Most spread-pricing profit was generated on prescriptions filled at non-affiliated pharmacies.
Source: FTC, "Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen," 6(b) Study (January 14, 2025).
Chart 4. The math fails at the counter
Cost to dispense versus typical PBM generic reimbursement
Independent pharmacies report a per-prescription dispensing cost of $12 to $15. PBM generic reimbursement frequently falls below the drug's acquisition cost, before any labor.
Cost to dispense (NCPA) Below-acquisition PBM reimbursement (illustrative)
Cost-to-dispense range ($12 to $15) is from the NCPA Cost of Dispensing study (NCPA is the trade association for independent pharmacies; funder disclosed). PBM reimbursement figure is illustrative. The FTC Second Interim Staff Report documents that Big 3 PBMs reimbursed unaffiliated pharmacies below acquisition cost on numerous generics; exact per-script averages are not reported.
Source: NCPA Cost of Dispensing study; FTC, "Second Interim Staff Report on Prescription Drug Middlemen" (January 14, 2025).
Chart 5. The closure curve has steepened
U.S. retail pharmacy count, 2015 to early 2026
The U.S. has roughly 8,000 fewer retail pharmacies than a decade ago, a 15% drop. More than 300 stores closed in the first 10 weeks of 2026 alone, on pace for the largest single-year decline of the post-2010 period.
Approximate U.S. retail pharmacy count
Approximate trajectory derived from AELP/NCPA aggregate reporting of an ~8,000-store net decline (about 15%) versus a decade earlier, anchored at endpoints. Mid-decade intermediate values are interpolated and should be treated as illustrative of the trend shape rather than precise annual counts.
Source: American Economic Liberties Project U.S. pharmacy closure tracker; NCPA Executive Updates (February 20 and March 6, 2026); Guadamuz et al., Health Affairs (December 2024) for the pre-2021 baseline.
