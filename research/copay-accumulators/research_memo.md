# The 11-State Enforcement Gap: Copay Accumulators in Apparent Violation of State Law

**Research memo — last updated May 2026**
**Source: AIDS Institute, "Our Loss, Their Gain: Copay Accumulator Adjustment Policies in 2025" (Feb 2025), full report on file**

## The headline you should probably lead with

**UnitedHealthcare is the named violator in 6 of the 11 states.** Per the AIDS Institute's 2025 plan-document review, UnitedHealthcare runs copay accumulator adjustment policies in apparent violation of state law in **Georgia, Louisiana, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Tennessee, and Washington**. This isn't a one-off compliance issue at a regional plan. It is a corporate-level pattern at the country's largest health insurer, in six different statutory regimes, sustained across plan years. No state insurance commissioner has, to my knowledge, brought a public enforcement action against UnitedHealthcare for this.

Other multi-state violators are smaller:

- **AmeriHealth Caritas Next** — Delaware and North Carolina
- **Moda Health** — Oregon and Texas
- **Medica** — Oklahoma (also flagged for CAAPs in non-violator states North Dakota, Iowa, Missouri, Kansas, Nebraska)

The single-state violators are: Anthem and Rocky Mountain Health Plans (Colorado), Ambetter from Peach State Health Plan (Georgia), MercyCare Health Plans (Illinois), BlueCross BlueShield of Tennessee (Tennessee), Sendero Health Plans (Texas).

## What the report actually is, and how it was made

The 2025 report is titled **"Our Loss, Their Gain: Copay Accumulator Adjustment Policies in 2025"** (February 2025). The authors are Stephanie Hengst (Manager, Policy & Research), Rachel Klein (Deputy Executive Director), and Michael Ruppal (Executive Director) at The AIDS Institute. **Funding disclosure to flag for editorial balance:** the report was supported by Genentech, Johnson & Johnson, PhRMA, AbbVie, and Pfizer — drug manufacturers whose copay-assistance programs are the value being captured by accumulator/maximizer schemes. The AIDS Institute states it maintains independence from corporate sponsors. You'll want to acknowledge this funding source in any reporting that relies on the data.

**Methodology:** The Institute reviewed individual marketplace health plans available for the 2025 plan year on the ACA exchanges in all 50 states and D.C. They examined policy documents from every insurance carrier offering a marketplace plan, looking for specific contract language about enrollee cost-sharing and copay accumulator policies. When policy documents were ambiguous or unavailable online, they called insurer customer-service lines to ask directly. **18 plans across 11 states did not make plan documents available online during open enrollment; of those 18, 13 had CAAPs based on customer-service phone confirmation.** That transparency failure is itself a reportable finding — one customer-service rep told the researchers that policy information would only be made available after a plan was purchased.

A plan was counted as having a CAAP if it refused to count copay assistance toward cost-sharing without limitation. Plans that counted assistance except for brand drugs with a medically-appropriate generic equivalent (which is permissible under the 2020 federal rule) were *not* counted as CAAP violators — these are flagged separately with an asterisk in the appendix.

## National context

- **More than 40% of all individual marketplace plans nationwide include a CAAP** (excluding the federally-permissible brand-with-generic carveout)
- **21 states + DC + Puerto Rico** have laws restricting CAAPs as of the 2025 plan year, protecting an estimated 26 million people (19% of US commercial lives, per Avalere)
- **In 13 of those 21 jurisdictions** (the 11 states below plus the federal-rule states Minnesota and Nevada), at least one plan still has CAAP language
- **HHS has explicitly declined to enforce** the 2020 federal rule even after the 2023 court ruling reinstated it. The agency announced plans to update the rule but has set no timeline.

## The 11 violator states — verified data

For each state below, the percentage is "% of plans with a CAAP" per the AIDS Institute's review. Bill citations are taken directly from Appendix 3 of the report (which reproduces the operative statutory language). Insurers marked with ✓ are the named violators per Appendix 1. Insurers with `*` apply CAAPs only to brand drugs with a generic equivalent (federally permissible — not counted as violators). Insurers with `+` use a "variable copay program" or third-party sourcing — a maximizer-style scheme worth flagging separately.

### 1. Colorado — 33.3% of plans (Grade C)

- **Bill:** SB.23 (2023). Codified in Colorado Revised Statutes (Title 10, Article 16) — exact section needs verification at DORA.
- **Effective:** 2025 plan year (the year the AIDS Institute reviewed)
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating a covered person's overall contribution to an out-of-pocket maximum or cost-sharing requirement under the covered person's health benefit plan, a carrier or PBM shall include any amount paid by the covered person or by another person on behalf of the covered person for a prescription drug" — with no generic exception for brand drugs accessed via prior auth/step therapy/appeals.
- **Regulator:** Colorado Department of Regulatory Agencies (DORA), Division of Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Anthem ✓, Cigna Healthcare *, Denver Health Plan, Kaiser Permanente, Rocky Mountain Health Plans ✓, SelectHealth
- **Named violators:** **Anthem, Rocky Mountain Health Plans** (2 of 6)
- **Reportable angle:** Colorado law just took effect for 2025 — these are *first-year* violations. Anthem (Elevance Health) is a Fortune 25 company; Rocky Mountain Health Plans is owned by UnitedHealth Group. So functionally Colorado is two large national carriers ignoring brand-new state law.

### 2. Delaware — 25.0% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** SB.267 (2022), codified at 18 Del. C. § 3566A
- **Effective:** 2023 plan year (per AIDS Institute grade table)
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating an enrollee contribution to any applicable cost sharing requirement, a carrier shall include any cost-sharing amounts paid by the enrollee or on behalf of the enrollee by another person." HSA carve-out for high-deductible plans until minimum deductible is met.
- **Regulator:** Delaware Department of Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Aetna CVS Health, Ambetter Health of Delaware, AmeriHealth Caritas Next ✓, Highmark Blue Cross Blue Shield Delaware
- **Named violator:** **AmeriHealth Caritas Next** (1 of 4)
- **Reportable angle:** Delaware is a small market — four carriers — making this very FOIA-able. AmeriHealth Caritas Next is owned by AmeriHealth Caritas, a Medicaid-focused managed care org. Worth asking the Delaware DOI specifically about AmeriHealth Caritas Next's 2023, 2024, and 2025 plan filings: were they reviewed for SB.267 compliance?

### 3. Georgia — 22.2% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** SB.313 (2020), codified at O.C.G.A. § 33-64-10
- **Effective:** 2021 plan year
- **Statutory language:** Has a generic carve-out — "a pharmacy benefits manager shall include any amount paid by the insured or paid on his or her behalf...for a prescription drug that does not have a generic equivalent or that has a generic equivalent but was obtained through prior authorization, a step therapy protocol, or the insurer's exceptions and appeals process."
- **Regulator:** Georgia Office of Insurance and Safety Fire Commissioner
- **Plans reviewed:** Aetna CVS Health, Alliant Health Plans, Ambetter from Peach State Health Plan ✓, Anthem Blue Cross and Blue Shield, CareSource, Cigna HealthCare of Georgia, Kaiser Permanente, Oscar Health Plan of Georgia, United Healthcare ✓
- **Named violators:** **Ambetter from Peach State Health Plan (Centene), United Healthcare** (2 of 9)
- **Reportable angle:** Georgia's law has been in effect for **five plan years** as of the 2025 review — the longest-standing non-enforcement case. Ambetter from Peach State is operated by Centene, the largest Medicaid managed-care company in the US. UnitedHealthcare is your headline cross-state pattern. Two of the largest national carriers, both publicly traded, both ignoring a five-year-old state law.

### 4. Illinois — 10.0% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** HB.0465 (2019). Codified in 215 ILCS 5 series — exact section to be verified at the Illinois General Assembly.
- **Effective:** 2020 plan year — **Illinois has had this law for six years**
- **Statutory language:** "A health care plan shall apply any third-party payments, financial assistance, discount, product vouchers, or any other reduction in out-of-pocket expenses made by or on behalf of such insured for prescription drugs toward a covered individual's deductible, copay, or cost-sharing responsibility, or out-of-pocket maximum." No generic exception in the operative language excerpted — Illinois appears to be in the broadest-coverage category.
- **Regulator:** Illinois Department of Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Aetna CVS Health *, Ambetter of Illinois, Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Illinois, Cigna Healthcare, Health Alliance, MercyCare Health Plans ✓, Molina Healthcare, Oscar Health Plan, Quartz, UnitedHealthcare
- **Named violator:** **MercyCare Health Plans** (1 of 10)
- **Reportable angle:** This is the longest-running enforcement gap of any state on the list — six years. MercyCare is a smaller regional plan (owned by Mercyhealth, a Wisconsin-Illinois health system), so the volume of affected patients is more limited than the UnitedHealthcare states. But the duration of non-compliance with a clear statutory mandate is the headline.

### 5. Louisiana — 25.0% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** SB.94 (2021). Note: this corrects the citation in earlier reporting that referenced "Act 263" — the report's Appendix 3 confirms the bill is **SB.94 of 2021**, codified in La. R.S. Title 22.
- **Effective:** 2022 plan year
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating an enrollee's contribution to any applicable cost-sharing requirement, a health insurance issuer shall include any cost-sharing amounts paid by the enrollee or on behalf of the enrollee by another person." This is broad-coverage language with no generic carve-out — Louisiana is one of the strictest jurisdictions.
- **Regulator:** Louisiana Department of Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Ambetter from Louisiana Healthcare Connections, Blue Cross Blue Shield of Louisiana, Louisiana Blue, CHRISTUS Health Plan, UnitedHealthcare ✓
- **Named violator:** **UnitedHealthcare** (1 of 5)
- **Reportable angle:** Louisiana's broad-coverage statute makes UnitedHealthcare's CAAP a clear violation — there's no generic-substitute argument available. Louisiana DOI Directive 257 already exists on PBM regulation; ask whether it has been used here.

### 6. North Carolina — 11.1% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** SB.257 (2021), the Medication Cost Transparency Act
- **Effective:** 2022 plan year
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating an insured's contribution to any out-of-pocket maximum, deductible, copayment, coinsurance, or other applicable cost-sharing requirement, the insurer or pharmacy benefits manager shall include any amounts paid by the insured, or on the insured's behalf, for a prescription that is either: (1) Without an AB-rated generic equivalent. (2) With an AB-rated generic equivalent if the insured has obtained authorization for the drug through any of the following: a. Prior authorization...b. A step therapy protocol. c. The exception or appeal process..."
- **Regulator:** North Carolina Department of Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Aetna CVS Health, Ambetter of North Carolina, AmeriHealth Caritas Next ✓, Blue Cross and Blue Shield of NC, CareSource, Cigna Healthcare, Oscar Health Plan of North Carolina, UnitedHealthcare ✓, Wellcare
- **Named violators:** **AmeriHealth Caritas Next, UnitedHealthcare** (per Appendix 1; the 11.1% report figure suggests one of these may be partially compliant — verify directly with the AIDS Institute)
- **Reportable angle:** AmeriHealth Caritas Next + UnitedHealthcare both running CAAPs in NC after the law has been in effect for three plan years. The Cystic Fibrosis Foundation publicly called for "full implementation" in November 2024 — they are an on-record source acknowledging that enforcement is lacking.

### 7. Oklahoma — 28.6% of plans (Grade C)

- **Bill:** HB.2678 (2021), codified at 36 O.S. § 1250.5(18) as an "unfair claim settlement practice"
- **Effective:** 2022 plan year (per AIDS Institute grade table; statute language took effect Nov 1, 2021)
- **Statutory language:** "Any of the following acts by an insurer, if committed in violation of Section 1250.3 of this title, constitutes an unfair claim settlement practice...18. As a health insurer that provides pharmacy benefits or a pharmacy benefits manager that administers pharmacy benefits for a health plan, failing to include any amount paid by an enrollee or on behalf of an enrollee by another person when calculating the enrollee's total contribution to an out-of-pocket maximum, deductible, copayment, coinsurance or other cost-sharing requirement."
- **Regulator:** Oklahoma Insurance Department; OID issued Bulletin LH 2021-05 to industry on this law
- **Plans reviewed:** Ambetter of Oklahoma, Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Oklahoma, CommunityCare, Medica ✓, Oscar Insurance Company, Taro Health Plan, UnitedHealthcare ✓
- **Named violators:** **Medica, UnitedHealthcare** (2 of 7)
- **Reportable angle:** **The strongest enforcement-gap case on this list.** Oklahoma codified the violation as an unfair claim settlement practice — a category with an existing civil-penalty regime (36 O.S. § 1250.16). This means OID has clear authority and a clear penalty mechanism. They have apparently used neither against Medica or UnitedHealthcare. Public administrative-order search at OID is the test.

### 8. Oregon — 16.7% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** HB.4113 (2024)
- **Effective:** 2025 plan year — first year of compliance review
- **Statutory language:** Has a generic carve-out (similar to North Carolina structure). "An insurer offering a health plan that provides pharmacy benefits and a pharmacy benefit manager shall include all amounts paid by an enrollee or paid by another person on behalf of an enrollee toward the cost of a covered prescription drug...if: (a) The drug does not have a generic equivalent; or (b) The drug has a generic equivalent and the enrollee has: (A) Obtained prior authorization...(B) Complied with a step therapy protocol; or (C) Received approval...through the insurer's...exceptions, appeal or review process."
- **Regulator:** Oregon Division of Financial Regulation (DCBS)
- **Plans reviewed:** BridgeSpan Health Company, Kaiser Permanente, Moda Health Plan ✓, PacificSource *, Providence Health Plan, Regence BlueCross BlueShield of Oregon
- **Named violator:** **Moda Health Plan** (1 of 6)
- **Reportable angle:** Oregon is the freshest narrative — law just took effect Jan 1, 2025. The Cystic Fibrosis Foundation called for "full implementation" in May 2024 specifically because the AIDS Institute's 2024 report had found 5 out of 6 Oregon plans had CAAPs. Now in 2025 the count has dropped to 1 of 6 — Moda Health — but that one is still in violation. The narrative writes itself: legislature passed a law with this specific evidence in hand, and one carrier is just continuing the practice.

### 9. Tennessee — 33.3% of plans (Grade C)

- **Bill:** HB.619 (2021), codified at Tenn. Code § 56-7-3205
- **Effective:** 2022 plan year (per AIDS Institute grade table; statute effective July 1, 2021)
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating an enrollee's contribution to an applicable cost sharing requirement, an insurer shall include cost sharing amounts paid by the enrollee or on behalf of the enrollee by another person. (b) Subsection (a) does not apply to a prescription drug for which there is a generic alternative, unless the enrollee has obtained access to the brand name prescription drug through prior authorization, a step therapy protocol, the insurer's exceptions and appeals process..."
- **Regulator:** Tennessee Department of Commerce and Insurance
- **Plans reviewed:** Alliant Health Plans, Ambetter of Tennessee *, BlueCross BlueShield of Tennessee ✓, Cigna Healthcare, Oscar Insurance Company, UnitedHealthcare ✓
- **Named violators:** **BlueCross BlueShield of Tennessee, UnitedHealthcare** (2 of 6)
- **Reportable angle:** **BCBS Tennessee is itself a story.** It's the dominant carrier in the state — one of the country's largest BCBS plans by market share — running a CAAP in apparent violation of state law for four plan years. The Coalition of State Rheumatology Organizations published a February 2025 brief titled "Patients in Tennessee Are Falling Through the Cracks." Pair the BCBS-TN angle with UHC for a state-level story showing the two largest carriers in non-compliance.

### 10. Texas — 13.3% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** HB.999 (2023), codified at Tex. Ins. Code § 1369.0542
- **Effective:** 2024 plan year (first plan year of compliance review per the law)
- **Statutory language:** Has a generic carve-out structure. "This section applies only to a reduction in out-of-pocket expenses made by or on behalf of an enrollee for a prescription drug...for which: (1) a generic equivalent does not exist; (2) a generic equivalent does exist but the enrollee has obtained access to the prescription drug under the enrollee's health benefit plan using: (A) a prior authorization process; (B) a step therapy protocol; or (C) the health benefit plan issuer's exceptions and appeals process; (3) an interchangeable biological product does not exist; or (4)..."
- **Regulator:** Texas Department of Insurance (TDI)
- **Plans reviewed:** Aetna CVS Health, Ambetter from Superior HealthPlan, Baylor Scott and White, BCBS Texas, CHRISTUS Health Plan, Cigna, Community First, Community Health Choice, Imperial Insurance Companies *, Moda Health ✓, Molina, Oscar, Sendero Health Plans ✓, UnitedHealthcare, Wellpoint
- **Named violators:** **Moda Health, Sendero Health Plans** (2 of 15)
- **Reportable angle:** Texas is a partial-improvement story. The 2024 AIDS Institute review (first year of HB.999) found 6 of 15 carriers (40%) had CAAPs. By 2025, that's down to 2 of 15 (13.3%). The law worked on most of the market; it didn't work on Moda Health and Sendero. Sendero is interesting: it's a "Local Nonprofit" carrier in Central Texas (Travis County) — small but flagrant. UnitedHealthcare is *not* on the Texas violator list, despite being on six other state lists.

### 11. Washington — 8.3% of plans (Grade B)

- **Bill:** SB.5610 (2022), codified at RCW 48.43.435
- **Effective:** 2023 plan year
- **Statutory language:** "When calculating an enrollee's contribution to any applicable cost-sharing or out-of-pocket maximum, a health carrier offering a non-grandfathered health plan with a pharmacy benefit, or a health care benefit manager administering benefits for the health carrier, shall include any cost-sharing amounts paid by the enrollee directly or on behalf of the enrollee by another person for a covered prescription drug that is: without a generic equivalent or with a generic equivalent that is preferred by the plan's formulary or enrollee has gained access via exceptions process and utilization management." Has an HSA carve-out.
- **Regulator:** Washington Office of the Insurance Commissioner (OIC)
- **Plans reviewed:** Ambetter (Coordinated Care) *, Asuris Northwest Health *, BridgeSpan Health Company *, Community Health Network of Washington, Kaiser Permanente, LifeWise Health Plan, Molina, Premera Blue Cross, Providence Health Plan, Regence BlueCross BlueShield *, Regence BlueShield *, UnitedHealthcare ✓
- **Named violator:** **UnitedHealthcare** (1 of 12)
- **Reportable angle:** Washington has the lowest violation rate of any state on the list (8.3%) — and the single violator is UnitedHealthcare. Washington's OIC is one of the more activist insurance commissioner offices in the country and publishes market-conduct exam reports openly at insurance.wa.gov/market-conduct-and-oversight. **Direct check:** pull every market conduct exam since 2023 and search for "accumulator" and "third-party payment." If UHC's compliance with RCW 48.43.435 has never been examined, that's the Washington-specific story.

## Beyond the 11 — adjacent findings worth knowing

The 2025 report flagged additional patterns adjacent to the 11-state story that may strengthen the article:

**The "variable copay program" / maximizer problem** — In 12 states (IN, MD, MO, MS, NE, NH, OH, PA, RI, UT, WI, WY), at least one plan uses a "variable copay program" that effectively captures the full annual value of manufacturer copay assistance without crediting any of it to the enrollee. The report calls this a maximizer-style scheme and notes "copay amounts are not available in plan materials" — patients can't even find out what they'll owe. None of these 12 states is on the 11-state violator list, but the practice is arguably a more aggressive form of the same harm.

**Alternative funding programs (AFPs)** — In Utah and Wyoming, plans only cover certain drugs if a third-party program cannot first secure the prescription from an alternative source (international purchasing, charity grants, etc.). The 2025 NBPP attempted to clarify this is impermissible because all covered drugs are EHB; Idaho and Montana plans changed their policies in response, but UT and WY plans persist.

**HHS non-enforcement of the federal rule** — The report explicitly states: "HHS has so far declined to enforce that rule." Combined with the absence of state enforcement actions in the 11 violator states, this creates a coordinated regulatory vacuum at both the state and federal levels.

**The transparency story** — 18 plans across 11 states (CA, FL, ID, KS, MD, MI, MO, NJ, NY, PA, SD) did not make policy documents available online during open enrollment. Of those 18, **13 turned out to have CAAPs** — when the AIDS Institute called customer service to ask. One representative told them plan info would only be made available after a plan was purchased.

## Cross-cutting story angles

**Angle A: The UnitedHealthcare investigation.** This is the strongest single thread. UnitedHealth Group is a Fortune 5 company with subsidiaries in every state. UnitedHealthcare-branded plans are running CAAPs in apparent violation of state law in six different statutory regimes, including Tennessee (4 years on the books), Oklahoma (codified as an unfair claim settlement practice), and Louisiana (broad-coverage statute, no generic carve-out). Pair this with: (1) UnitedHealth Group's recent regulatory and political headwinds (DOJ antitrust scrutiny, Senate Finance Committee PBM hearings); (2) Optum Rx, UnitedHealth's PBM, which is the entity actually running these accumulators; (3) statements from UnitedHealthcare on the public record about its commitment to compliance, contrasted with the AIDS Institute findings.

**Angle B: The five-year non-enforcement story (Illinois, Georgia).** Illinois has had a copay-counts law since 2020 (six plan years). Georgia since 2021 (five plan years). Plans continue to run CAAPs. State insurance commissioners have brought no public enforcement action against any named carrier. This is a story about state regulatory capture, under-resourcing, or both.

**Angle C: The freshest case (Oregon).** Legislature passed HB.4113 in 2024 specifically because the AIDS Institute documented that 5 of 6 Oregon plans had CAAPs. Law took effect Jan 1, 2025. By Feb 2025 review, Moda Health was still doing it. This is the cleanest "law-on-the-books, ignored on day one" narrative.

**Angle D: The structural ERISA gap.** Even where state laws *do* work, they don't reach self-funded employer plans — which is the majority of the commercial market. Iowa SF 383 was the first state attempt to enforce a copay accumulator ban against the self-funded ERISA market; watch for federal litigation over preemption. The 11-state non-enforcement story is also a preview of what happens if Congress passes the federal HELP Copays Act and HHS does not enforce it — which the AIDS Institute notes HHS has already declined to do for the existing 2020 rule.
