# Article Outline — "The Copay Loophole States Tried to Close"

**Working title (pick one or remix):**
- *The Copay Loophole States Tried to Close — and Insurers Walked Right Through*
- *Twenty-One States Banned This. Major Insurers Are Still Doing It.*
- *The Law on the Books, the Practice in the Pharmacy: Inside the Copay-Accumulator Enforcement Gap*

**Target length:** 1,000–1,200 words (5-minute read)
**Format reminders:** one chart per major section, one number per section, voice = skeptical-but-fair, structural framing not single-villain, the **enforcement vacuum** is the thesis.

---

## 1. Lead (75–100 words)

The hook needs to puncture the framing that "states have already solved this."

Suggested opening:
> Twenty-one states, the District of Columbia, and Puerto Rico have laws on the books requiring health insurers to count drug-manufacturer copay assistance toward a patient's deductible and out-of-pocket maximum. They were passed with bipartisan support, in response to documented patient harm, with backing from advocacy groups for HIV, multiple sclerosis, cystic fibrosis, and rheumatic disease patients. In eleven of those states, major national insurers are running the practice anyway. Not one state insurance commissioner has, on the public record, brought an enforcement action. The laws exist. The enforcement does not.

**One number for the section:** 11 states with documented violations and zero public enforcement actions.

**Source:** [The AIDS Institute, *Our Loss, Their Gain: Copay Accumulator Adjustment Policies in 2025*](https://aidsinstitute.net/documents/TAI-2025-Report.pdf) — funded by Genentech, J&J, PhRMA, AbbVie, Pfizer (disclose).

## 2. The Story So Far (100 words)

Quick recap so newer readers aren't lost. Don't dwell.

Cover:
- A copay accumulator (CAAP) is a plan or PBM policy that accepts manufacturer copay assistance but refuses to credit it to the patient's deductible — patient pays for the drug twice.
- HHS first restricted CAAPs in the 2020 NBPP, reversed itself in the 2021 NBPP, was sued by patient groups, and lost in *HIV+Hepatitis Policy Institute v. HHS* (D.D.C., Sept. 29, 2023). DOJ withdrew its appeal Jan. 16, 2024. The 2020 rule is operative; HHS has declined to enforce it.
- States filled the vacuum. The first laws passed in 2019; the most recent (Oregon HB.4113) took effect Jan. 1, 2025.

**One number for the section:** ~26 million people (19% of US commercial lives) are nominally protected by state copay-counts laws.

**Sources:**
- [HIV+Hepatitis Policy Institute v. HHS — Georgetown Litigation Tracker](https://litigationtracker.law.georgetown.edu/litigation/hiv-and-hepatitis-policy-institute-et-al-v-hhs-et-al-docket-no-23-05284-d-c-cir-nov-30-2023/)
- [Avalere — State Copay Accumulator Bans Will Affect 19% of US Commercial Lives](https://avalere.com/insights/state-copay-accumulator-bans-impact-11-of-us-commercial-lives)
- [Drug Channels — Copay Accumulator and Maximizer Update](https://www.drugchannels.net/2024/02/copay-accumulator-and-maximizer-update.html)

## 3. What the Laws Actually Say (175 words)

The structure of the bans, in two paragraphs.

Cover:

**The standard prohibition.** "When calculating an enrollee's contribution to any applicable cost-sharing requirement, the insurer shall include any amounts paid by the enrollee or on behalf of the enrollee by another person." That's the operative sentence in roughly half the state statutes — Connecticut, Delaware, Illinois, Louisiana, New Mexico, New York, Oklahoma, Virginia, West Virginia, Puerto Rico — with no exception. The patient's copay assistance counts, period.

**The federal carve-out states.** The other half (Arizona, Arkansas, Colorado, DC, Georgia, Kentucky, Maine, North Carolina, Oregon, Tennessee, Texas, Vermont, Washington) match the 2020 federal rule: assistance must count *unless* the drug has a medically appropriate generic equivalent that the patient hasn't obtained access to via prior authorization, step therapy, or appeals. The carve-out is narrow. Specialty drugs without generics — the medications most likely to involve copay assistance in the first place — are squarely covered.

**Oklahoma's structural choice worth noting.** OK codified the violation as an "unfair claim settlement practice" (36 O.S. § 1250.5(18)), which slots compliance into an existing civil-penalty enforcement regime. It's the most enforceable version of the law. It is not, as we'll see, being enforced.

**One number for the section:** 21 state statutes, plus DC and PR.

**Visual:** **Chart 1 — Map of state copay-counts laws by category.** Three colors: broad ban (no carve-out), generic carve-out, no law. Mark the 11 violator states.

**Sources:**

- [Connecticut OLR Report 2024-R-0034](https://www.cga.ct.gov/2024/rpt/pdf/2024-R-0034.pdf) — best comparative summary.

## 4. The Compliance Gap (250 words)

The investigative core. Don't list every carrier; show the pattern.

Cover:

**The AIDS Institute's 2025 review** of marketplace plan documents in all 50 states found that in 11 states with copay-counts laws — Colorado, Delaware, Georgia, Illinois, Louisiana, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Oregon, Tennessee, Texas, and Washington — at least one major insurer is still running CAAPs in plan documents. The Institute's methodology is replicable: pull the 2025 marketplace plan policy documents, search for the contract language, call customer service when the documents are unclear.

**The corporate-parent pattern.** UnitedHealthcare appears as a named violator in six of those states (Georgia, Louisiana, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Tennessee, Washington). UnitedHealth Group's smaller subsidiary Rocky Mountain Health Plans is on the list in Colorado, where Anthem (Elevance Health) and Centene's Ambetter brand are also flagged. AmeriHealth Caritas Next appears in Delaware and North Carolina. BlueCross BlueShield of Tennessee — the dominant carrier in its state — is on the list. Moda Health is flagged in Oregon and Texas. The named violators include some of the country's largest publicly traded health insurers.

**Texas as the cleanest test case.** When HB.999 took effect for the 2024 plan year, the AIDS Institute's first-year review found 6 of 15 Texas marketplace insurers (40%) had CAAPs. By the 2025 review, that was down to 2 (Moda Health and Sendero Health Plans, an Austin-area nonprofit). The law moved the market — except for the carriers that ignored it.

**Illinois as the hardest case.** Illinois HB.0465 took effect in 2020. Six plan years later, MercyCare Health Plans is still running a CAAP. No state insurance department, in any of the 11 states, has filed a public enforcement action.

**One number for the section:** UnitedHealthcare, named violator in 6 states.

**Visual:** **Chart 2 — Named violator carriers by state.** Stacked bars: each of 11 states across the x-axis; named carriers stacked, with corporate parent color-coded.

## 5. What It Costs the Patient (150 words)

The patient-impact paragraph. Use the AIDS Institute's own worked example.

Cover:
- Without a CAAP, a patient on a $1,680/month specialty drug ($20,160 annual list) with $7,200 in manufacturer copay assistance and a $4,600 deductible / $8,550 out-of-pocket maximum hits their OOP cap in August. They pay $1,350 out of pocket. The insurer collects $8,550.
- With a CAAP on the same drug, the same plan, the same patient: the manufacturer's $7,200 doesn't count toward the deductible. The patient is forced to cover the deductible separately, then coinsurance, until they hit the OOP cap *months later*. They pay **$7,960** out of pocket. The insurer collects $15,160 — nearly double.
- And many patients can't pay. IQVIA: when out-of-pocket costs at the pharmacy counter hit $250, more than 70% of patients walk away empty-handed.

**One number for the section:** $7,960 — what an accumulator costs one patient on one drug.

**Visual:** **Chart 3 — Patient cost with vs. without a CAAP.** Adapt the AIDS Institute's Example 2 spreadsheet (page 30 of the 2025 report) into a clean two-line chart over twelve months.

**Sources:**
- [AIDS Institute 2025 report, Appendix 2 (Example 2)](https://aidsinstitute.net/documents/TAI-2025-Report.pdf)
- IQVIA prescription abandonment data (cited in the AIDS Institute report endnote 2)

## 6. The Deeper Loophole (125 words)

The structural problem that explains why even *more* enforcement won't fully solve this.

Cover:
- ERISA preempts state insurance law for self-funded employer plans, which cover the majority of the commercial market. State copay-counts laws don't reach those plans.
- Iowa SF 383 in 2024 became the first state attempt to apply a copay-accumulator ban to self-funded ERISA plans. Federal preemption litigation is likely.
- Even where state laws *do* apply, plans are escalating to "maximizers" — different mechanism, same result. PBMs partner with vendors like SaveOnSP (Express Scripts) and PrudentRx (CVS Caremark) to reclassify specialty drugs as "non-essential health benefits" and capture the full annual value of manufacturer assistance. The 2025 NBPP attempted to close this loophole; the AIDS Institute documents 12 states where it isn't yet closed.

**One number for the section:** ~80% of large employer plans are self-funded and therefore beyond state reach.

**Sources:**
- [Drug Channels — How Copay Accumulators and Maximizers Have Changed Payers' View of Copay Support](https://www.drugchannels.net/2022/07/how-copay-accumulators-and-maximizers.html)
- [Johnson & Johnson v. SaveOnSP complaint (D.N.J.)](https://kffhealthnews.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2022/11/31-1-002.pdf)

## 7. What to Watch (100 words)

The "what's next for readers who want to stay smart" close. Three questions.

1. **Does any state insurance commissioner act?** A market-conduct exam, consent order, or administrative penalty in any of the 11 states would be the first public enforcement on record. Texas (newest law) and Oklahoma (UCSPA framework) are the cleanest tests.
2. **Does HHS resume enforcement of the 2020 federal rule?** The agency has declined to act since the 2023 court order reinstated it. Watch for the announced new rulemaking — and its timeline.
3. **Does Congress move on the HELP Copays Act?** The bill (S.1375/HR.830 in the 118th Congress) would extend the rule to self-funded ERISA plans and is expected to be reintroduced. Bipartisan support exists; a vote does not.

End with a one-sentence kicker.

> **The bottom line.** A patchwork of state laws exists. A federal rule technically exists. None of it works in the eleven states where it most needs to.
