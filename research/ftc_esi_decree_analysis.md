# Reading the FTC v. Express Scripts Consent Decree (D9437)

## Tribune research note — Apr 26, 2026

This is a section-by-section walkthrough of the *Decision and Order as to Respondents Express Scripts, Inc., Evernorth Health, Inc., Medco Health Services, Inc., and Ascent Health Services LLC* — the 15-page consent decree the FTC issued on February 4, 2026.

The headlines say the FTC "blew up" the PBM model and that ESI is on the hook for $7B in patient savings over 10 years. That's directionally true but it papers over what the order actually requires. Reading the decree itself, three things jump out that aren't being said clearly in the law-firm and trade-press writeups:

1. **The decree only binds ESI/Evernorth/Medco/Ascent.** Caremark/Zinc and OptumRx/Emisar are separate respondents in the same case (D9437) and are *not* bound by this order. Caremark moved to settle on March 23, 2026 with terms not yet final. OptumRx is still negotiating.
2. **Section XI ("Meeting Competition") is the entire ballgame.** Sections II, III, IV, V, and VIII — almost everything that matters — can be opted out of by any plan sponsor in writing. The order doesn't change ESI's behavior; it changes ESI's *default* offering. Whether the market shifts depends on whether plan sponsors choose the new default or write themselves out of it. The single hard lock-in is Cigna Healthcare's own fully-insured plans, which by the decree's express terms cannot opt out of Section II.
3. **Section III (TrumpRx) is paper.** It's conditional on Congress or CMS exempting direct-to-consumer purchases from Medicaid Best Price (Section 1927) and 340B price calculations. Until that statutory or regulatory carve-out happens, the entire TrumpRx provision is dormant. The press is reporting it as a real provision; it isn't yet.

Those three points together are the spine of the Tribune piece I'd write.

## Section-by-section, what the order actually requires

**Findings + Definitions (pp. 1–6).** Standard preamble. Worth noting: "Respondent" is the four-entity ESI bloc only. The definition of **"Rebate"** explicitly excludes "manufacturer administrative fees, data fees, and other fees for administrative services" — this is the original loophole that birthed Ascent (rebates re-labeled as fees). Section VI later closes it, but the definition itself preserves the historical distinction.

**§ I — Non-discrimination of Low-WAC versions.** ESI cannot put a high-list-price version of a drug on a Standard Formulary while excluding, tiering up, or restricting (PA/step therapy) a lower-list-price version of the same drug from the same manufacturer. Effective by Implementation Date (Jan 1, 2027). *Caveat:* the manufacturer has to offer the Low-WAC version at a net unit cost ≤ the High-WAC version, and supply it adequately. So if a manufacturer launches a low-list-price version but doesn't price it competitively net-of-rebate, ESI can keep favoring the high-list version. The order only works if the manufacturer plays along. (This is the Humira biosimilar problem, encoded.)

**§ II — Member out-of-pocket capped at Net Unit Cost.** Patient cost-share for any covered drug cannot exceed list-price-minus-rebate. Applies to Cigna's fully-insured book *automatically* via the agreement with Connecticut General Corporation, *and* applies to ESI's Standard Offering for plan sponsors. **This is the actual mechanism that produces the $7B in claimed savings** — it stops members on high-deductible plans from paying full list price on rebated drugs. The savings flow through HDHP members on rebated branded drugs (insulin chief among them). Effective by Jan 1, 2027.

**§ III — Standard offering for TrumpRx access.** ESI must include "covered access to TrumpRx" — but only "provided legislative or regulatory changes are made to exempt direct-to-consumer purchases from the calculation of rebates under the Medicaid Drug Rebate Program in Section 1927 of the Social Security Act and Section 340B." This is the suspension trigger. Until Congress passes a Best Price/340B carve-out for DTC, manufacturers can't offer steep DTC discounts without dragging down their Medicaid pricing. Until that happens, **§ III is non-operative**. The order references EO 14,297 from May 12, 2025 and TrumpRx as live infrastructure, but the contractual obligation is contingent.

**§ IV — Insulin Patient Assurance Program.** When a plan sponsor adopts a formulary that includes a PAP-eligible insulin product, all members get full PAP access (the $25/$35-style cap depending on the product) unless the plan sponsor opts out in writing. PAP cost-share for insulin must always be lower than what the member's regular pharmacy benefit plan would charge. Effective by Implementation Date.

**§ V — Standard offering on compensation and rebates.** By Jan 1, 2028, ESI's Standard Offering must (A) pass rebates to members at point of sale, (B) not include rebate guarantees to plan sponsors, and (C) not employ spread pricing. **All three are opt-out under § XI.** This is the section that supposedly "ends the rebate game," but in practice it ends rebate-game-as-default, not rebate-game-as-option.

**§ VI — Compensation cannot be tied to list price.** As of Implementation Date, no compensation ESI receives from manufacturers — including the admin/data/service fees historically used to launder rebates through Ascent — can be "based, directly or indirectly, on the List Price of any Drug Product or any related benchmark." This is the structural change. It actually closes the Ascent loophole at the math level: even if you call something a "data fee," it cannot be a percentage of WAC. Notably, **§ VI applies to all of ESI's manufacturer compensation**, not just the Standard Offering. There's no opt-out for this one. **This is the most consequential single sentence in the decree** and it's the part the trade press has paid least attention to.

**§ VII — Increased transparency for plan sponsors.** Annual cost reporting, claim-level reporting, Transparency in Coverage compliance support, and full disclosure of any compensation paid to consultants or brokers ("we paid your broker $X to recommend us"). By Jan 1, 2028. This last point is quietly significant — broker conflicts in PBM selection are a known but rarely-quantified problem, and the disclosure requirement could reshape how mid-market employers buy PBM services.

**§ VIII — Standard offering to retail community pharmacies.** By Jan 1, 2028: (A) cost-plus reimbursement (acquisition cost + dispensing fee), provided the pharmacy submits quarterly acquisition-cost data; (B) additional payment for non-dispensing services (immunizations, MTM, screenings, etc.); (C) any willing pharmacy that accepts the Standard Offering must be admitted. **Opt-out applies here too** (§ XI.B) — pharmacies can negotiate non-standard terms. The "any willing pharmacy" provision is a long-time NCPA priority and a real win for independent pharmacies. The quarterly cost-data requirement is operationally heavy for one-store independents and worth a pharmacy-perspective story on its own.

**§ IX — Mandatory marketing of the Standard Offerings.** ESI must spend at least **$10M/year for 5 years** advertising and promoting the new Standard Offerings, must clearly disclose their availability in any RFP response or marketing material, cannot disparage them, and cannot coerce plan sponsors or pharmacies away from them. *This is unusual.* Most consent decrees don't impose affirmative marketing obligations. The FTC clearly anticipated that ESI's natural commercial response would be to bury the Standard Offering at higher prices and steer customers to non-compliant alternatives — § IX exists to prevent that. It signals how seriously the FTC took the loophole risk.

**§ X — Reshoring and GPO Safe Harbor.** By July 1, 2028, all Ascent activities, employees, functions, and assets used for rebate negotiation/contracting move from Switzerland (Mühlentalstrasse 36, Schaffhausen — the address is in the order) to the U.S. Additionally, any rebate GPO ESI owns or controls must comply with the **GPO Safe Harbor at 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952** under the Anti-Kickback Statute — meaning written agreement with each participant, fee disclosure, and the standard 3%-or-disclosed-fee structure. PBM-owned GPOs have historically argued they aren't subject to the AKS GPO safe harbor because they're not "real" GPOs. **The decree forecloses that argument prospectively for ESI.** This is meaningful because it sets a template the FTC can extend to Caremark/Zinc and OptumRx/Emisar.

**§ XI — Meeting Competition. The whole ballgame.** Sections II, III, IV, V, and VIII can all be opted out of via written request from a plan sponsor (or, for § VIII, a community pharmacy). ESI must offer the Standard Offering and obtain written acknowledgement that the plan sponsor read Exhibit A — but after that, anything goes. **One exception, and it's small but important: this section "shall not apply with respect to Cigna Healthcare's Fully Insured Health Plans."** Cigna's fully-insured book is locked into § II. The much larger self-insured employer market is fully opt-out. Estimate: roughly two-thirds of large-employer covered lives are in self-funded plans. Self-insured plans are exactly the population most likely to opt out, because they have the financial incentive and the broker infrastructure to do so.

The acknowledgement form (Exhibit A) is one page, plain English, and basically says "the FTC made us tell you the Standard Offering would benefit your members, you've chosen something else, please sign." It's a friction step, not a barrier.

**§ XII — Monitor.** A monitor approved by the FTC's Bureau of Competition and Bureau of Consumer Protection serves for **3 years post-Implementation** (not 10). Reports to ESI's Chief Legal Officer, with escalation to the Board of Directors if the CLO doesn't act. Annual reports to the FTC. The Chairman can terminate the monitor at any time if "no longer necessary." This is a notably permissive monitorship structure — the monitor's authority is real but ESI has substantial control over scope, and the Chairman's unilateral termination authority is a potential weakness.

**§ XIII — Compliance reports.** Interim reports every 90 days until Implementation Date, then annual. Document retention 5 years.

**§ XIV — Change in respondent.** 30-day notification before dissolution, M&A, or any other change "that might affect compliance obligations." This is the standard hook to keep the order alive through corporate restructuring.

**§ XV — FTC access.** Five business days' written notice; access to facilities, records, ESI; right to interview officers and employees. Standard.

**§ XVI — Cooperation with continuing litigation. The competitor-pressure weapon.** ESI must cooperate with any FTC litigation "relating to or arising out of the allegations in the Complaint... against any other person." Specifically, ESI must produce up to **5 additional employees for deposition** and **3 employees to testify at trial** in cases against Caremark, OptumRx, or anyone else — at ESI's own expense. This is the FTC turning ESI into a witness against its competitors. **This provision is materially relevant to why CVS folded six weeks later** and why OptumRx is still negotiating: the moment ESI signed, its emails, internal communications, and employee testimony became available to the FTC's case against the other two. They were now suing not just the FTC but also a settled co-defendant who is required to help.

**§ XVII — Term.** 10 years from Implementation Date.

**Exhibit A.** The mandatory disclosure to plan sponsors who opt out. One page. Highlights §§ I and II benefits in plain English. Asks for written acknowledgement. Worth noting it does *not* describe the broker-disclosure or community-pharmacy provisions in the disclosure form itself, only the formulary and member-cost-share pieces.

## What the press is underplaying

**1. The Cigna fully-insured carve-in is the only true lock.** Every commentary I've seen describes § XI as "a loophole" without mentioning that Cigna's fully-insured book is hard-wired to § II by the proviso ("this Section XI shall not apply with respect to Cigna Healthcare's Fully Insured Health Plans"). That's not a loophole; that's a contract between the FTC and Cigna's own insurance arm to deliver some of the savings to its own customers regardless of what plan sponsors choose. Question worth chasing: how big is Cigna's fully-insured book relative to ESI's total covered lives, and does the $7B savings estimate assume self-insured plans opt in or out? The FTC has not published the underlying assumption. (Their Analysis to Aid Public Comment is the place to look for the model.)

**2. § VI is the structural change, not § V.** The clean version of the FTC win is § VI: ESI's compensation from manufacturers cannot be based on list price, and there is no opt-out. Everything else in the order can be sidestepped through § XI. § VI cannot. This is the part that actually breaks the historical math of the rebate game, because rebates as % of WAC have been the engine of list-price inflation. Re-read § VI carefully with that frame.

**3. § XVI is a litigation lever, not housekeeping.** Most coverage treats the cooperation clause as boilerplate. It isn't. It made ESI a tool the FTC could point at Caremark and OptumRx, with a contractual obligation to produce specific numbers of witnesses. That's leverage, and it likely accelerated the other two settlements.

**4. TrumpRx in § III is contingent — and the contingency is non-trivial.** Until Congress or CMS exempts DTC sales from Best Price/340B calculations, TrumpRx cannot scale because manufacturers will not give deep DTC discounts that ratchet down their Medicaid prices. The decree references this explicitly. The story to chase: where does that statutory change stand? Is there a bill? An NPRM? If neither, § III is dormant indefinitely and the "TrumpRx is part of the settlement" framing is misleading.

**5. The $10M/year mandatory marketing spend signals the FTC's own loophole concern.** The agency knew § XI was porous and built in $50M of forced advertising to combat it. That's a tell. The Tribune piece can use this as evidence that the FTC itself is uncertain how transformative the order will be in practice.

**6. The GPO Safe Harbor compliance requirement (§ X.B) is more important than the reshore.** Reshoring Ascent is symbolically powerful — it gets headlines about $750B of "purchasing activity" returning to the U.S. — but the substantively binding piece is § X.B, which forces Ascent (and any future ESI-owned rebate GPO) to comply with 42 C.F.R. § 1001.952. That's the AKS safe harbor with real disclosure obligations. The reshore could be cosmetic; the safe harbor compliance is procedural law.

## What's no longer "asymmetric" about the asymmetric-settlement angle

When I pitched this story before reading the decree, I framed it as ESI folding while CVS and Optum kept fighting. As of the most recent docket activity:

- **ESI/Ascent**: settled Feb 4, 2026. Order on public comment until March 16, 2026.
- **Caremark/Zinc**: joint motion to withdraw from adjudication filed March 23, 2026. Settlement terms not yet public, but expected to mirror the ESI order.
- **OptumRx/Emisar**: stay extended March 3, 2026; "significant progress" reported in negotiations.

So the angle is no longer "why did one fold first" — by the time the Tribune publishes, all three will likely be settled or close to it. The sharper angle is **"what does the ESI order tell us about the deal CVS just took and the deal Optum is negotiating?"** Specifically:

- Did Caremark get a different § XI? (Watch for whether CVS Caremark's settlement has the same opt-out architecture.)
- Did Caremark get a different reshoring deadline for Zinc? Zinc isn't offshore the same way Ascent is — does Caremark have to "reshore" anything, or just adopt the Safe Harbor?
- Will Optum's eventual deal include the same § XVI cooperation clause, and if so, against whom — given there's no fourth Big PBM?
- Whether the monitorship structure is identical (3 years post-Implementation, ESI-CLO reporting) or whether CVS/Optum negotiated different oversight.

These are answerable questions with public dockets. The Tribune piece writes itself once Caremark's order publishes (likely by mid-2026) — a comparison table between the three orders, with the differences as the story.

## Suggested Tribune issue framing

A 5-minute read with this structure:

- **Lead:** the FTC didn't blow up the PBM model — it made the existing model opt-in.
- **What the decree actually requires** — the four pieces with no opt-out: § VI (no list-price-tied compensation), § X (Ascent reshore + Safe Harbor compliance), § IX ($10M/year marketing mandate), and § II for Cigna's fully-insured book.
- **What's optional** — § XI in plain English, with the math of why most self-insured plan sponsors will likely opt out unless ESI prices the Standard Offering competitively.
- **One chart:** % of ESI covered lives by self-insured vs. fully-insured (Cigna's only locked-in segment), to size what fraction of ESI's book is hard-wired to the new rules.
- **One number:** the $7B savings estimate, with the caveat that the FTC has not published the opt-out assumption underlying it.
- **The kicker:** what to watch when Caremark's order publishes — the comparison points listed above.

That structure delivers what the Tribune promises: short, balanced quant + qual, a take, and reader takeaway about something that hasn't been laid out clearly elsewhere.

## Caveats and things to verify before publishing

- The opt-out reality of § XI is a *prediction*, not a fact. Some plan sponsors may genuinely prefer the Standard Offering. Sourcing this point should include 1–2 actual benefits consultants who advise self-insured employers.
- The "Cigna fully-insured book is the only hard lock" framing assumes Cigna does not later restructure that book. Cigna would have to notify FTC under § XIV before doing so, but the order doesn't prohibit re-segmentation.
- The TrumpRx dormancy claim depends on the current state of Best Price / 340B regulation. Verify against the most recent CMS rulemaking (the KFF readthrough on TrumpRx is the cleanest non-partisan source) before publishing.
- The "$750B purchasing activity reshoring" figure comes from the FTC press release, not the decree itself. The decree just says the activities, employees, functions, and assets move to the U.S. The dollar figure is the FTC's characterization and worth verifying or at least attributing.
- Ascent's actual rebate volume isn't disclosed in the decree. Public estimates are extrapolations from Cigna 10-Ks. Treat any specific Ascent dollar figure with appropriate hedging.
